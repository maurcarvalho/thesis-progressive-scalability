\section{G3: Design for Progressive Scalability}
\label{sec:g3-design-progressive-scalability}

This section presents G3, which focuses on designing modular monoliths so that scalability is achieved progressively rather than through premature distribution. In this dissertation, \emph{progressive scalability} is defined as the capacity of a modular monolith to accommodate increasing load, data volume, and organizational growth through a deliberate sequence of architectural interventions, each proportional to the observed need, without requiring a wholesale migration to a distributed architecture. G3 operationalizes this capacity by identifying module-level scale points, introducing asynchronous boundaries where load demands diverge, and abstracting infrastructure dependencies so that extraction remains a controlled option rather than an emergency.

G3 completes the Architectural Design Dimension (G1--G3). While G1 enforces modular boundaries and G2 preserves maintainability through bounded cost of change, G3 ensures that the resulting modular structure can absorb growth without collapsing into either a monolithic bottleneck or a premature microservices decomposition. Together, these three guidelines establish the structural foundation upon which the Operational Fit, Organizational Alignment, and Guideline Orientation dimensions build.

\subsection*{Intent and Rationale}

The prevailing narrative in cloud-native literature frames scalability as an inherently distributed concern: when the system must scale, it must be decomposed into independently deployable services~\cite{arya2024beyond, blinowski2022monolithic}. This framing creates a false dichotomy. Teams are implicitly told to choose between a monolith that cannot scale and microservices that can, ignoring the intermediate architectural states that a well-modularized monolith can occupy.

In practice, most early-stage systems do not face uniform scaling pressure. Load concentrates in specific modules, specific use cases exhibit disproportionate growth, and specific data domains expand faster than others. A system designed for progressive scalability acknowledges this unevenness and provides mechanisms to address it surgically.

G3 therefore treats scalability as a \emph{gradient} rather than a binary switch. The guideline defines a four-level Progressive Scalability Spectrum (Table~\ref{tab:scalability-spectrum}): \emph{Tune} (vertical optimization), \emph{Decouple} (asynchronous boundaries), \emph{Isolate} (data separation), and \emph{Extract} (selective service extraction) as a last resort. Each level is triggered by observable metrics rather than speculative anticipation, and each intervention is reversible if conditions change.

\subsection*{Conceptual Overview}

Progressive scalability rests on the principle that both synchronous and asynchronous communication channels are available from the initial deployment. The choice between them is driven by observed load patterns, and the transition from one to the other is a configuration change. Module-level metrics (throughput profiles, latency variance, data ownership) are collected continuously and evaluated against defined thresholds, removing subjectivity from scaling decisions. These structural properties correspond to the Key Principles defined below.

\subsection*{Applicability Conditions and Scope}

G3 applies to modular monolith systems that satisfy the following conditions:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item Module boundaries are enforced and dependencies are explicit, as established by G1.
  \item Maintainability discipline is in place, ensuring that contracts are stable and change locality is preserved, as established by G2.
  \item The system is expected to grow in load, data volume, or team size, but the timing and distribution of that growth are uncertain.
\end{itemize}

G3 does not prescribe specific cloud providers, container orchestrators, or scaling technologies. Its scope is limited to architectural preparation: the design decisions and structural properties that make scaling interventions feasible, proportional, and reversible. G3's scalability metrics, particularly the extraction readiness score, provide the quantitative trigger for G4 (Migration Readiness), which addresses how to prepare a specific module for independent deployment once evidence warrants it.


\subsection*{Objectives}

\emph{Research contributions:}
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{Formalize scalability as a gradient:} Define a progression spectrum (Tune, Decouple, Isolate, Extract) that replaces the binary monolith-or-microservices framing with a sequence of proportional, reversible interventions.
  \item \emph{Quantify progression triggers:} Establish module-level metrics (throughput divergence, sync ratio, data ownership, abstraction coverage) that determine when a module should transition to the next spectrum level.
  \item \emph{Provide a composite extraction readiness metric:} Synthesize boundary health (G1), maintainability signals (G2), and scalability preparation (G3) into a per-module score that grounds Extract-level decisions in architectural evidence.
\end{itemize}

\emph{Implementation objectives:}
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{Embed scalability infrastructure from inception:} Design the architecture so that the infrastructure for all spectrum levels (caching, async channels, schema isolation) is present from the initial deployment.
  \item \emph{Abstract infrastructure as a replaceable dependency:} Ensure modules interact with infrastructure through ports or interfaces that can be re-bound across spectrum levels without modifying domain logic.
  \item \emph{Preserve extraction optionality:} Maintain the ability to extract any bounded context as an independent service while positioning extraction as the last resort.
  \item \emph{Record progression decisions:} Document each spectrum-level transition as an Architecture Decision Record (ADR) that captures the trigger condition, intervention, expected outcome, and rollback path.
\end{itemize}


\subsection*{Key Principles}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{Scale where it hurts, not where it might:}
  Scaling interventions are driven by observed bottlenecks, not speculative anticipation. Module-level metrics provide the evidence base.

  \item \emph{Asynchronous boundaries as scalability seams:}
  The boundary between two modules becomes a scalability seam when their throughput requirements diverge. An asynchronous channel at this seam absorbs load spikes without propagating backpressure to the consumer.

  \item \emph{Data isolation from project inception:}
  Each module owns a dedicated database schema established at project start. Cross-module data access is mediated exclusively through published contracts, never through direct table queries.

  \item \emph{Infrastructure as a replaceable dependency:}
  Modules depend on infrastructure through ports rather than concrete implementations. When a module must move from an in-process event bus to a distributed broker, the change is confined to the adapter, not the domain logic.

  \item \emph{Every intervention is reversible by design:}
  Each spectrum-level transition preserves the option to retreat. Infrastructure abstractions and communication channels are structured so that rolling back requires configuration changes, not architectural rework.

  \item \emph{Extraction is the last resort, not the first reflex:}
  Extracting a module introduces network boundaries, distributed failure modes, and operational overhead. G3 positions extraction at the end of the spectrum, after vertical optimization, async decoupling, and data isolation have been exhausted.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Implementation Mechanisms}

G3 is implemented through four architectural mechanisms, all established at project start:

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{Module-scoped resource profiling:} Each module's resource consumption is attributable through instrumentation or naming conventions. Concrete instrumentation is demonstrated in G6 (Observability Patterns); G3 defines the architectural requirement.

  \item \emph{Communication abstraction layer:} Inter-module communication passes through a contract-based abstraction (event bus, command dispatcher) that can be backed by in-process dispatch, an in-memory queue, or a distributed broker such as Apache Kafka without changing the caller or handler.

  \item \emph{Persistence port pattern:} Each module accesses its data through repository interfaces backed by a dedicated database schema declared via \texttt{pgSchema()}. When a module reaches the Extract level, the adapter is re-bound to a dedicated database instance without changing domain logic.

  \item \emph{Scalability decision records:} Each spectrum-level transition is recorded in an ADR that captures the trigger condition, intervention, expected outcome, and rollback path. The Tiny Store reference implementation does not include an ADR example because all four modules remain at the Tune level; the first ADR will be produced during the verification phase (Chapter~5).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Common Failure Modes and Anti-Patterns}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Premature Distribution}: extraction attempted before readiness threshold ($\varepsilon_m < 0.8$)\\
  A team extracts a module into its own service because "it handles the most traffic," despite low throughput divergence and incomplete schema isolation. Re-embedding the module later requires undoing infrastructure, CI, and data ownership changes~\cite{gravanis2021dont, su2024from}.

  \item \textbf{Uniform Scaling Assumption}: throughput divergence ignored ($\Delta\Theta$ not assessed)\\
  All modules are deployed behind identical auto-scaling rules, even though one handles 10$\times$ the traffic of the others. Underloaded modules waste resources while the overloaded one remains constrained.

  \item \textbf{Shared Database as Implicit Coupling}: data ownership incomplete ($\omega < 1.0$)\\
  One module runs \texttt{SELECT} queries directly against another module's tables (e.g., querying \texttt{order\_items} to compute reorder thresholds). When the owning module refactors its schema, the dependent module's queries break silently, and neither can be extracted independently.

  \item \textbf{Synchronous Call Chains Under Load}: sync ratio exceeds threshold ($\rho_{\mathrm{sync}} > 0.3$)\\
  A workflow calls multiple modules synchronously in sequence: validate stock, then charge payment, then create shipment. A latency spike in any downstream module blocks the entire chain, propagating failures upstream to the customer.

  \item \textbf{Infrastructure Lock-In}: abstraction coverage incomplete ($\alpha < 1.0$)\\
  Domain services call \texttt{redis.get()} and \texttt{kafka.produce()} directly instead of using adapter interfaces. Transitioning from Tune to Decouple requires rewriting business logic to swap the infrastructure provider.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The Scalability Readiness Gap}

G1 and G2 ensure that module boundaries are correct and sustainable. Yet a system can satisfy both guidelines and still be \emph{unable to scale proportionally}. A module with perfect boundary discipline and stable contracts may still share a database schema with three other modules, communicate exclusively through synchronous calls, and depend on infrastructure through concrete implementations. When load arrives, scaling requires a coordinated migration rather than a surgical intervention. G3's metrics close this gap by making infrastructure preparation, communication flexibility, and data ownership visible and measurable.

\medskip
\noindent
\emph{Formal definitions.} In prose, metrics are referred to by name; the notation below supports unambiguous tooling implementation. Runtime metrics are tracked through the observability infrastructure defined in G6.

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Module Throughput Profile ($\Theta_m$).}
  Measures the request-per-second spread across modules; a wide gap signals uneven load.
  \[
    \Delta\Theta = \max_{m \in M} \Theta_m - \min_{m \in M} \Theta_m
  \]
  where $M$ = set of modules in the system, $\Theta_m$ = requests per second handled by module $m$.\\
  \emph{Target:} Monitor; trigger Decouple when divergence exceeds 2:1 ratio between connected modules~\cite{richardson2018microservices}.

  \item \textbf{Module Latency Variance ($\sigma^2_{L,m}$).}
  Measures response-time consistency for a single module under load.
  \[
    \sigma^2_{L,m} = \mathbb{E}[(L_m - \mu_{L,m})^2]
  \]
  where $L_m$ = observed response time for module $m$, $\mu_{L,m}$ = mean response time for module $m$.\\
  \emph{Target:} Monitor; investigate when variance increases $>2\times$ baseline~\cite{richardson2018microservices}.

  \item \textbf{Cross-Module Synchronous Call Ratio ($\rho_{\mathrm{sync}}$).}
  Measures the proportion of inter-module calls that block the caller until completion.
  \[
    \rho_{\mathrm{sync}} = \frac{|I_{\mathrm{sync}}|}{|I_{\mathrm{sync}}| + |I_{\mathrm{async}}|}
  \]
  where $I_{\mathrm{sync}}$ = set of synchronous inter-module calls, $I_{\mathrm{async}}$ = set of asynchronous inter-module calls.\\
  \emph{Target:} $\rho_{\mathrm{sync}} \leq 0.3$~\cite{dragoni2017microservices}.

  \item \textbf{Data Ownership Clarity Index ($\omega$).}
  Measures the fraction of database tables owned by exactly one module.
  \[
    \omega = \frac{|T_{\mathrm{single\text{-}owner}}|}{|T|}
  \]
  where $T$ = set of all database tables, $T_{\mathrm{single\text{-}owner}}$ = tables accessed by exactly one module.\\
  \emph{Target:} $\omega = 1.0$~\cite{evans2003ddd}.

  \item \textbf{Infrastructure Abstraction Coverage ($\alpha$).}
  Measures the fraction of infrastructure access points mediated through ports or interfaces.
  \[
    \alpha = \frac{|A_{\mathrm{abstracted}}|}{|A|}
  \]
  where $A$ = set of all infrastructure access points (databases, caches, queues, external APIs), $A_{\mathrm{abstracted}}$ = access points mediated through a port or interface.\\
  \emph{Target:} $\alpha = 1.0$~\cite{FordParsons2017}.

  \item \textbf{Extraction Readiness Score ($\varepsilon_m$).}
  Synthesizes G1 boundary health, G2 maintainability, and G3 scalability signals into a per-module readiness score.
  \[
    \varepsilon_m = 0.25 \cdot b_m + 0.20 \cdot (1 - \rho_{\mathrm{sync}}^{(m)}) + 0.15 \cdot a_m + 0.20 \cdot \omega_m + 0.20 \cdot v_m
  \]
  where $b_m$ = boundary compliance (G1), $(1 - \rho_{\mathrm{sync}}^{(m)})$ = async readiness, $a_m$ = Anti-Corruption Layer coverage (G2), $\omega_m$ = schema isolation, $v_m$ = event contract versioning.
  \emph{Target:} $\varepsilon_m \geq 0.8$ before Extract~\cite{wolfart2021modernizing}. Weights are proposed defaults reflecting relative extraction risk; teams should calibrate them. Validation through practitioner interviews is outlined in Chapter~5.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Compliance summary.} G3 compliance holds when:
\[
  \rho_{\mathrm{sync}} \leq 0.3 \land \omega = 1 \land \alpha = 1 \land \varepsilon_m \geq 0.8 \text{ for extraction candidates}
\]


\subsection*{Documentation Guidelines}
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{Module Scale Profile:}
  Record each module's current spectrum level, observed throughput profile, and known scaling constraints. Update when monitoring data changes or a transition occurs.

  \item \emph{Scalability ADRs:}
  Record each spectrum-level transition: trigger condition, intervention applied, expected outcome, rollback path, and actual outcome.

  \item \emph{Infrastructure Dependency Map:}
  Document which infrastructure dependencies each module uses and whether they are accessed through abstractions ($\alpha = 1.0$) or directly.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Tooling Capabilities Checklist}
Any tool supporting progressive scalability should address:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item Module-scoped metrics collection (throughput, latency per module).
  \item Communication abstraction (sync/async through the same contract interface).
  \item Schema ownership analysis (identify tables accessed by multiple modules).
  \item Load simulation (targeted per-module or per-path testing).
  \item Extraction readiness assessment (composite G1+G2+G3 score per module).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Literature Support Commentary}

The academic literature on software scalability exhibits a persistent structural gap. Empirical studies on microservices consistently provide evidence on horizontal scaling but frame distribution as a prerequisite for scalability rather than one option among several~\cite{arya2024beyond, blinowski2022monolithic, berry2024isItWorth}. Comparative analyses demonstrate that the trade-offs are context-dependent, yet stop short of proposing what a team should do \emph{between} monolith and microservices~\cite{jatkiewicz2023differences}. Studies advocating for monoliths acknowledge that monolithic architectures can serve moderate scaling needs but do not formalize how scaling should be approached incrementally~\cite{montesi2021sliceable, gravanis2021dont, deLauretis2019from}. Industry experience reports reinforce this pattern: documented retreats from microservices~\cite{segment2023}, accounts of premature distribution's operational toll~\cite{auth02019}, and arguments that monoliths remain viable at significant scale~\cite{medium2019} all suggest that distribution is not a universal solution. Research on accelerating early-stage development through monolithic architectures further supports deferring distribution until evidence warrants it~\cite{lima2024accelerating}.

Two proposals come closest to the progressive scalability concept. The ``sliceable monolith'' keeps the system unified until a slice needs independent deployment~\cite{montesi2021sliceable}, but focuses on deployment granularity rather than the intermediate interventions (async boundaries, data isolation, infrastructure abstraction) that precede extraction. The Strangler Fig pattern~\cite{stranglerPatterns} offers incremental migration but operates at the application boundary rather than addressing internal modular preparation. The evolutionary architecture perspective provides the philosophical foundation~\cite{FordParsons2017} but does not operationalize it for scaling decisions at the module level.

A systematic review of the SLR corpus reveals that no existing work provides: (1) a formalized intermediate-state model between monolith and microservices; (2) quantified progression triggers through measurable metrics; or (3) a composite extraction readiness metric synthesizing signals across guidelines. G3 addresses all three gaps.

\subsection*{Core Contribution}

The contribution is not the individual scaling techniques (caching, messaging, schema isolation, extraction are well-established) but a unified decision model that organizes them into an evidence-based progression:

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=8pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \emph{A formalized scalability gradient.} The Progressive Scalability Spectrum replaces the binary ``monolith or microservices'' framing with four intermediate states (Tune, Decouple, Isolate, Extract), each with explicit metric triggers and rollback paths.

  \item \emph{Metric-driven progression triggers.} Each transition is governed by quantifiable thresholds that remove subjectivity. The metrics are composable across guidelines: G3 builds on G1's boundary health and G2's maintainability signals.

  \item \emph{Per-module, reversible interventions.} Scaling decisions are scoped to individual modules and each intervention is reversible through adapter abstractions, preserving architectural optionality~\cite{gravanis2021dont, segment2023}.
\end{enumerate}

The scalability levels defined here provide the trigger criteria for G4 (Migration Readiness), which prepares individual modules for extraction when the evidence warrants it.

\subsection*{G3 Applied: Progressive Scalability in the Tiny Store}
\label{sec:g3-applied}

The following sections demonstrate the progressive scalability infrastructure in the Tiny Store reference implementation.\footnote{Full source: \url{https://github.com/maurcarvalho/tiny-store}}

\subsubsection*{Tune: Module-Scoped Caching}

The \texttt{CacheService} enforces module-namespaced keys through a \texttt{buildKey()} method, ensuring cache entries are isolated per bounded context. An \texttt{InMemoryCacheAdapter} serves development; a \texttt{RedisCacheAdapter} is injected for production:

\needspace{4\baselineskip}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript,caption={CacheService with module-namespaced keys (cache.service.ts)},label={lst:g3-cache-service}]
class CacheService {
  private adapter: CacheAdapter;
  private globalPrefix: string;

  private buildKey(module: string, key: string): string {
    return `${this.globalPrefix}:${module}:${key}`;
  }

  async get<T>(module: string, key: string): Promise<T | null> {
    const raw = await this.adapter.get(
      this.buildKey(module, key));
    return raw ? JSON.parse(raw) as T : null;
  }

  async set<T>(module: string, key: string, value: T,
               ttlSeconds?: number): Promise<void> {
    await this.adapter.set(this.buildKey(module, key),
      JSON.stringify(value), ttlSeconds);
  }
}
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
A read-through caching decorator applies this pattern declaratively. When Tune-level optimization is needed, the team adds \texttt{@Cacheable('inventory', 60, (sku) => `product:\$\{sku\}`)} to the target method.\footnote{Full decorator: \texttt{libs/shared/infrastructure/src/cache/cache.decorator.ts}}

\subsubsection*{Decouple: Module-Scoped Queues}

The \texttt{QueueService} provides module-scoped queues following the \texttt{module:purpose} naming convention, with the same adapter pattern as the cache layer:

\needspace{4\baselineskip}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript,caption={QueueService with adapter pattern (queue.service.ts)},label={lst:g3-queue-service}]
class QueueService {
  private adapter: QueueAdapter;

  async enqueue<T>(queue: string, data: T,
                   options?: JobOptions): Promise<Job<T>> {
    return this.adapter.enqueue(queue, data, options);
  }

  registerWorker<T>(queue: string,
                    handler: WorkerHandler<T>): void {
    this.adapter.registerWorker(queue, handler);
  }
}
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
The \texttt{InMemoryQueueAdapter} ships with the initial deployment; a \texttt{BullMQAdapter} backed by Redis is injected when \texttt{REDIS\_URL} is defined.\footnote{Adapters: \texttt{libs/shared/infrastructure/src/queue/adapters/}}  The composition root selects the adapter:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript,caption={Queue adapter selection (queue.factory.ts)},label={lst:g3-queue-factory}]
function createQueueService(): QueueService {
  const adapter = process.env.REDIS_URL
    ? new BullMQAdapter(process.env.REDIS_URL)
    : new InMemoryQueueAdapter();
  return new QueueService(adapter);
}
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
Removing \texttt{REDIS\_URL} reverts the entire queue infrastructure to in-memory dispatch. The same pattern applies to the event bus: at the Tune level, events are dispatched in-process; when a module pair triggers the Decouple threshold, the event bus switches to a Kafka topic using the same event contracts. The caller publishes an event; the adapter decides whether it is dispatched in-memory or written to a Kafka partition.

\subsubsection*{Isolate: Per-Module PostgreSQL Schemas}

Each module declares its own PostgreSQL schema using Drizzle ORM's \texttt{pgSchema()} API, co-located with the module source:

\needspace{4\baselineskip}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=TypeScript,caption={Per-module PostgreSQL schema isolation (inventory/db/schema.ts)},label={lst:g3-schema-isolation}]
import { pgSchema, text, integer, timestamp }
  from 'drizzle-orm/pg-core';

const inventorySchema = pgSchema('inventory');

export const productsTable = inventorySchema.table('products', {
  id: text('id').primaryKey(),
  sku: text('sku').notNull(),
  name: text('name').notNull(),
  stockQuantity: integer('stock_quantity').notNull(),
  reservedQuantity: integer('reserved_quantity').notNull(),
  status: text('status').notNull(),
  createdAt: timestamp('created_at').notNull(),
  updatedAt: timestamp('updated_at').notNull(),
});
// orders, payments, shipments follow the same pattern
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
Because \texttt{pgSchema()} is the single source of truth, no central configuration is required. Drizzle qualifies all queries with the schema prefix (e.g., \texttt{inventory.products}), making cross-schema access impossible through the ORM layer. When Extract-level extraction occurs, the module changes its connection string to a dedicated instance; schema declarations, repositories, and domain logic remain unchanged.

When monitoring signals (connection pool saturation, lock wait times, replication lag) indicate that a module needs a dedicated database instance, the transition is itself progressive: provision the instance and rebind the connection string. The schema, tables, and queries remain identical.

\subsubsection*{Extract: What Extraction Looks Like}

Extract is reached only when Tune, Decouple, and Isolate have been exhausted for a specific module. At this point, the module is already decoupled asynchronously and owns its data exclusively. Extraction is a topology change: the domain logic, repository interfaces, and event handlers remain unchanged. The Tiny Store repository includes a working example (\texttt{docker-compose.l3.yml})\footnote{Full file: \url{https://github.com/maurcarvalho/tiny-store/blob/main/docker-compose.l3.yml}} that deploys Orders as an independent service:

\needspace{4\baselineskip}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=yaml,caption={Extract-level topology (docker-compose.l3.yml, abbreviated)},label={lst:g3-l3-extraction}]
services:
  orders-service:  # own API (port 3001) + own DB + own events
    build: { context: ., dockerfile: apps/orders-service/Dockerfile }
    ports: ['3001:3001']
    environment:
      DATABASE_URL: postgres://orders:orders@orders-db:5432/orders
      REDIS_HOST: redis
    depends_on:
      orders-db: { condition: service_healthy }

  orders-db:
    image: postgres:16-alpine
    environment:
      POSTGRES_DB: orders
      POSTGRES_USER: orders

  monolith:  # serves inventory, payments, shipments on port 3000
    environment:
      DATABASE_URL: postgres://tinystore:tinystore@main-db:5432/tinystore
      EXTRACTED_MODULES: orders  # middleware returns 410 Gone
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
In production, the monolith and extracted service communicate through Kafka topics; event contracts remain identical. Three infrastructure changes occur, none touching domain logic:

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=4pt,topsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{DATABASE\_URL} points to a dedicated instance; schema and queries are identical.
  \item Event listeners are scoped to Orders-related events in the service's own entry point.
  \item The service runs in its own container with independent deployment pipeline.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The operational concerns of extraction (saga orchestration, event versioning, deployment pipelines) are addressed in G4 and G5. G3 establishes the structural preparation; G4 and G5 address the operational execution.

\subsection*{Tutorial: Step-by-Step Application}

\subsubsection*{Step 1: Verify Infrastructure Abstractions}

\needspace{4\baselineskip}
\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash,caption={Check for direct infrastructure imports},label={lst:g3-tut-alpha}]

\subsubsection*{Exercise 1: Synchronous Cross-Module Call (sync ratio $\uparrow$)}

Modify \texttt{create-shipment/service.ts} to import and call \texttt{GetPaymentHandler} from the payments module:

\subsubsection*{Exercise 2: Shared Table Access (data ownership $\downarrow$)}

\subsection*{Conclusion}

G3 demonstrated that scalability in a modular monolith is a gradient traversed through proportional interventions: vertical optimization before async decoupling, async decoupling before data isolation, data isolation before extraction. The Tiny Store reference implementation shows that infrastructure for all four levels can be embedded from the initial deployment, making scalability a structural property rather than a future migration.

With boundaries enforced (G1), maintainability preserved (G2), and scalability infrastructure prepared (G3), the architecture is structurally ready for growth. G4 addresses the next concern: when a module's extraction readiness score warrants the Extract level, how to prepare it for independent deployment through event-driven contracts, saga orchestration, and controlled topology changes.
